# Counting Into Existence

### A finitist reading of the primes and the sieve

Allen Proxmire · July 2026

> **What this is.** A *philosophical interpretation*, not a mathematical claim. Nothing here changes which numbers are prime, or any theorem in this collection — it reframes what a prime *is*. Read it the way you'd read Bohm next to Copenhagen: same predictions, different ontology. Tagged throughout **[stance]** (a position taken), **[fact]** (established), **[open]** (honest debt).

---

## 1. The thesis

**[stance]** There are no completed infinities anywhere in reality. There are only as many numbers as there have been **events** — and probably far fewer, since almost every event re-instantiates a small number rather than minting a new one. Numbers are **potentially** infinite (there is never a last one) but never **actually** infinite (there is never *all* of them, at once, as a finished set).

This is potential infinity in Aristotle's sense, and it is old and respectable, not fringe: Gauss refused "the use of infinite magnitude as something completed"; Brouwer and Weyl built mathematics on it; Zeilberger and Nelson defend strict finitism today.

## 2. The boundary is accrued, not chosen

**[stance]** The standard objection to finitism is *"where is the last number?"* — name any bound $N$ and one can conceive $N+1$. The answer here is that the boundary is **not stipulated, it is accrued**: as long as there are more events, there is a bigger number; never a last one, never a completed all. The edge of the numbers *moves with history*. There is no cutoff to defend because there is no cutoff — only a growing frontier.

**[fact]** And it is physically bounded. Lloyd's *computational capacity of the universe* caps the elementary operations performable since the Big Bang at $\sim 10^{120}$ (on $\sim 10^{90}$ bits); the Bekenstein/holographic bound caps the information in any finite region. That $10^{120}$ already includes all the Planck-time updating over all of history — it is the "crystallized" maximum, every degree of freedom committing every tick. The real universe is mostly inert most of the time, so the actual count of commitment-events sits well beneath it.

## 3. Numbers are acts, not stored objects

**[stance]** A number is a **commitment** — an event — not a thing that persists. This dissolves the worry *"how would the universe remember the count?"*: it doesn't need to. Events do not require a ledger to have happened (the past occurred whether or not anything records it). "How many numbers" is therefore not a register reality maintains; it is a **fact about history** — how far the arrow has gotten — and history needs no rememberer.

## 4. The sieve at the speed of influence

**[stance]** The Sieve of Eratosthenes hides exactly one completed infinity, and it is easy to miss. When a prime $p$ activates at $p^2$, the textbook removes the whole arithmetic progression $\{p^2, p^2+p, p^2+2p, \dots\}$ — an infinite set, struck out *all at once*. That single **"all at once"** is the only actual infinity in the construction.

Replace it with a **wavefront moving at a finite speed** — the speed of *influence*, not instantaneous action at a distance — and the infinity is gone. At any tick, $p$ has struck only finitely many multiples, and it is **never done**. The sieve stops being a finished object and becomes a process forever mid-stride.

Three consequences follow:

- **A light-cone on the integers.** Each prime's influence spreads forward from $p^2$. A number's primality is not a fact waiting to be read; it is **settled only once every sweep that can reach it has arrived**.
- **The $\sqrt n$ horizon becomes causal.** That $n$ is determined by primes up to $\sqrt n$ is not merely a computational shortcut — those are the only sweeps that have had *time* to reach $n$. The parity barrier (the one proven wall of the finite sieve) reads as a **causal horizon**, not just an epistemic one.
- **Primality is an event, not an eternal property.** "Is $n$ prime?" has no answer until the wavefronts arrive — as a measurement has no outcome until it happens. Even the primes are not out there waiting; they are *committed*, one arriving front at a time.

## 5. The honest ledger

The whole point of writing this down is to keep the stance and its costs in the same place.

- **[fact] It is interpretation, not arithmetic.** It cannot and does not change which numbers are prime. It changes what one takes a prime to *be* — an event in a causal process rather than an entry in an eternal register. Same clicks, different ontology.
- **[open] The speed is a postulate.** For the ontology, "finite" is the entire content and does all the work (kills the infinity, installs causality); *no numerical value is needed.* The moment one tries to **pin** the speed — to make it reproduce the $\sqrt n$ horizon exactly, or to give it a value in Planck units — one is at the **numerology cliff**, where a coherent interpretation degrades into a forced coincidence. Kept qualitative, the reading is safe; quantified, every digit must be earned.
- **[open] The potentialist's bill.** Rereading every "for all primes" as **schematic** — a rule that applies to whatever is instantiated, not a survey of a finished totality — works (it is exactly Brouwer–Weyl), but it costs the law of the excluded middle over infinite domains. "There are infinitely many twin primes, or there are not" stops being automatically true. The Twin-Prime Bertrand Postulate becomes a statement about *how far the sieve will go*, not about a completed object — which is how it has been treated here all along.
- **[open] Why the infinite fiction pays rent.** The strongest card against finitism remains unpaid: infinite mathematics *works* — assume infinitely many primes, do analysis over the reals, and correctly predict finite physical facts. The finitist owes an account of why pretending there are infinities gives right answers about a finite world. The standing reply — the infinite is a *lossless-enough compression* — is coherent but a promissory note.

## 6. One roof

Read together with the rest of this collection, the stance is not a separate philosophy bolted on; it is the same primitive seen from outside the mathematics. The **sieve is the clock**; primes are how far it has ticked; twin primes exist as far as it has reached; the **wheel** is the frozen history of its commitments; and the count needs no keeper because it was never a thing — only a history. No completed infinities anywhere: just a wavefront of commitments, and the integers being **counted into existence at the speed of influence**.
